Since a few years I have heard a new mantra in the mouth of multiple managers: let's appoint external consultants to improve the performance of the team or the company. At the beginning, I thought it was a good idea. But the results have been disappointing, especially long term. I want to go through a few scenarios that I have witnessed in the last few years.

Worse, sometimes multiples consultants will get hired, and put in a sort of god like team that is supposed to solve all the issues. That team will be better paid, has more visibility, and better management sponsorship.

# Introducing the new consultant

First, it is the way this is presented to the team. I have heard sentences from a manager that sounded like: "I hired a new consultant that is starting next monday, that person is excellent, and will upskill your team and show you how to deliver properly, increase the quality of your work, and increase the teams's velocity".

Most of the time, the team did not ask for help. They are caught by surprise: a new team member to onboard on monday ... that was not planned.

The message to the team is disastrous. Most of the time, productivity issues have been discussed in team retrospectives. The team knows what the underlying issues are. But instead of listening to the team, that manager decided to find a different root cause: the team has not been capable of fixing it's issues.
Some managers will percieve that some teams have a low productivity. But compared to what? This happens a lot when managers change from one company to another: if they were thriving in a high velocity green field startup, and move to a company with more legacy systems, they will think performance and agility is low. It maybe is, but the team members probably have something to say about that.

# Snowflakes

Of course, the new consultant has it's conditions to work with the company. Why would they come to work in that low-performing environment. Sometimes it feels it's a disgrace for them to work with other team members.

So special conditions are written in the contract: more remote work (or even full remote), whereas team members get quibbled about they work presence at the office. The daily rate is set high, and this informatins is told many times to the team: that snowflake is expensive, because they are an expert that has more knowledge than team members. On the other hand, team members got low raises over the last raises (remember, the team performance is bad).

This creates a form of jealousy from the get go, and a lot of frustration.

Repeat this multiple times, and managers wonder why internal staff is demotivated and lack engagement.

# The solo player

Often, as the new consultant can't be put inside the team due to the hierarchical organisation, they will be put somewhere else in the hierarchy. So they will get a shiny title like "staff engineer" or "delivery manager". Sometimes that person in introduced to the teams as having more decision power than the team. Sometimes their role is never really defined and they float through the organisation without any clear boundaries.

Most consultants won't try to fix this situation. The power this gives them is huge. I have seen staff engineers gatekeep a teams work, practically slowing them down.

# The god like team

When you have one problem, why not multiply it. Let's say you have multiple underperformign teams: if you have enough budget, you can probably justify adding a few more of these positions to your company. Maybe form a team of super-consultants.

I have seen these teams: "Delivery teams", "Staff engineer team". Sometimes they are hidden inside a platform team, because it's a natural place to put them, as the platform team has a huge transverse impact. Or maybe create a separate team to work on a project, to show how well they will delivery it (on time, with high quality).
